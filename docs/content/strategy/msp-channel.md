# MSP / Partner Channel Strategy

## Current stance

MSPs still look like one of the strongest routes to SMB and mid-market buyers that run frontline-heavy operations.

The key correction from the product audit is this: the **channel thesis is strong**, but the **partner motion should still be described as developing rather than fully productized**.

That means we should keep building the channel story while being careful not to imply that NFR policy, partner operations, onboarding flows, and reseller packaging are already fully mature.

## Why MSPs still matter

- MSPs already serve the same SMB and mid-market buyers that fit BlokSec best.
- Many of those clients struggle with password resets, shared devices, turnover, and compliance pressure.
- MSPs can understand the operational pain quickly because they are often the team absorbing the support burden.
- BlokSec gives them a differentiated story where phone-based MFA and generic identity messaging are weak.

## What MSPs can credibly sell today

### 1. Frontline/shared-device access
The clearest partner story is still shared-device and no-smartphone authentication for frontline teams.

### 2. Multi-protocol stack fit
BlokSec can fit into standards-based environments where OIDC, SAML, and WS-Federation matter.

### 3. Admin, audit, and reporting value
The product has more admin and reporting maturity than the current public GTM fully reflects.

### 4. Broader platform value beyond MFA
Where relevant, the BlokSafe launchpad, credential-sharing workflows, and auditability story make the platform more than a narrow login tool.

## What should still be treated as qualification-heavy

- formal NFR policy
- self-serve trial expectations
- partner portal or deal registration workflows
- repeatable white-label operating playbooks
- large-scale reseller packaging and support boundaries
- fully proven MSP deployment motion across multiple partner types

## Value proposition to MSPs

### Operational value
- lower password-reset and access-friction burden
- a cleaner story for shared-device accountability
- fewer ad hoc credential-sharing workarounds

### Commercial value
- a differentiated security and access-management service line
- stronger conversations in compliance-sensitive industries
- a more concrete frontline wedge than generic passwordless positioning alone

## Recommended near-term partner motion

### Referral first
Use this for firms that can introduce the right buyers but do not yet need deeper operational support.

### Design-partner or pilot reseller second
Use this for a small number of partners willing to help shape the onboarding, proof, and operating model.

### Broader reseller motion later
Expand only after NFR, onboarding, support, economics, and deployment workflows are better documented.

## Best-fit partner profile

- serves SMB or mid-market clients in manufacturing, retail, warehousing/logistics, food processing, or care settings
- already manages Microsoft, Google, or mixed application environments
- feels the pain of reset-heavy support and shared-device access problems
- can sell compliance, accountability, and workflow-fit outcomes

## Program elements to formalize before a larger push

- one documented tier model
- NFR and trial policy
- partner onboarding and deployment checklist
- stack-fit guidance for Microsoft, Google, and standards-based app environments
- competitive responses for Entra QR + PIN, Duo, OLOID, and related alternatives
- campaign-in-a-box assets for the first two verticals
- validated ROI assumptions that match the approved evidence set

## Immediate recommendation

Keep the partner program simple and operationally realistic.

The near-term goal is to win a small number of strong-fit partners and learn from real deployments, not to pretend the channel model is already fully industrialized.
